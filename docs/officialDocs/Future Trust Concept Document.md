# Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation Future Trust Concept

## Planning Draft

## Standard Definitions

**Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation** means the broader family legacy mission and the intended future trust structure created to build, protect, organize, and preserve family assets for current and future generations. Unless a trust agreement has been legally created and executed, the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation refers to the mission and planning framework only—not an existing legal trust or charitable entity.

**Justice McNeal LLC** means the current Georgia single-member limited liability company owned and controlled by Justin McNeal. Justice McNeal LLC currently serves as the operating and administrative entity that collects contributions, maintains records, manages approved expenses, and supports the early mission stage of the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation.

**Intended future trust** means the family trust that the family plans to create in the future, currently referred to by the working name Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation. The final legal name, form, and terms must be determined through professionally drafted trust documents and attorney review.

**Contributor** means a person who voluntarily contributes funds through Justice McNeal LLC in support of the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation mission. A Contributor is not a legal owner, LLC member, trustee, beneficiary, manager, or decision-maker unless separate legally binding documents say otherwise.

**Active Legacy Contributor** means a Contributor who maintains an active recurring monthly contribution through the approved contribution system under the Contribution Policy. This status describes mission participation only. It does not create ownership, equity, repayment rights, withdrawal rights, voting rights, trustee rights, beneficiary rights, or guaranteed financial returns.

**Separate legacy entities** include Justice McNeal LLC, Karry Kraze, future property-holding LLCs, and other businesses or holding entities. Each entity has its own purpose and records. They are not automatically combined unless formally connected by legal documents. In the intended future structure, the intended future trust may own or control some or all of these entities.

**Planning document notice:** This document describes current intentions and operating concepts only. It is not legal, tax, investment, estate planning, or financial advice, and it does not create binding legal rights by itself.

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document explains the intended future trust structure for the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation.

The purpose of this document is to organize the current vision, goals, structure, roles, and questions related to the creation of the intended future trust. This document is intended to help prepare for professional legal, tax, estate planning, and financial review.

This document is not a legally created trust agreement. It is not intended to replace legal advice, tax advice, estate planning advice, financial advice, an LLC operating agreement, or any professionally drafted trust document.

For the broader mission, values, and current structure, see the *Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation Charter*. For current LLC operations, see the *Justice McNeal LLC Operating Notes*.

## 2. Intended Trust Name

The intended trust name is:

**Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation**

The name Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation is intended to represent the broader family legacy mission and the intended future trust structure created to build, protect, organize, and preserve family assets for current and future generations.

The final legal name of the trust should be reviewed by an attorney to determine whether “Foundation” is appropriate for a private family trust structure or whether a name such as “Justice McNeal Legacy Trust” or “Justice McNeal Family Legacy Trust” would be legally clearer.

## 3. Reason for Creating the Intended Future Trust

The intended future trust would exist to create a long-term structure for generational wealth, asset protection, family organization, ownership planning, and legacy preservation.

The planned purpose of the intended future trust is to help organize and protect assets in a way that can continue beyond any one individual. The intended future trust is intended to support long-term family goals such as investing, land ownership, business ownership, education, property ownership, and opportunities for future generations.

The intended future trust should help separate personal ownership from family legacy ownership. Instead of assets being passed down directly to individuals in a way that could become divided, sold, misused, or lost, the goal is for certain assets to remain owned or controlled by the intended future trust or by entities owned by the intended future trust.

## 4. Relationship to Justice McNeal LLC

Justice McNeal LLC is intended to be one of the operating entities connected to the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation.

Justice McNeal LLC currently serves as the operating entity used to collect contributions, manage the website, maintain records, coordinate legacy-related activities, and support the early mission stage administered through Justice McNeal LLC.

In the future, the intended future trust may own or control Justice McNeal LLC. If that occurs, Justice McNeal LLC may continue to serve as the contribution-collection, administration, events, website, and family participation entity under the broader intended future trust structure.

Justice McNeal LLC may provide access, updates, events, resources, or other mission-related benefits to Active Legacy Contributors. These benefits are mission-participation benefits only and should not create LLC ownership, trust ownership, guaranteed returns, repayment rights, or legal claims to trust assets unless otherwise stated in legally binding documents.

For current LLC operating details, contribution collection, and administration, see the *Justice McNeal LLC Operating Notes* and the *Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation Contribution Policy*.

## 5. Relationship to Other Entities

The intended future trust may eventually own or control multiple entities that serve different purposes within the family legacy structure.

These entities may include Justice McNeal LLC, Karry Kraze, future property-holding LLCs, investment-related entities, or other family-owned or family-controlled businesses.

Justice McNeal LLC, Karry Kraze, and future property-holding LLCs are separate entities with distinct purposes unless formally connected by legal documents. Each entity should have a clear purpose, separate records, separate accounts when appropriate, and proper legal documentation.

The intended future trust may serve as the top-level ownership or control structure, while each LLC or entity operates for a specific purpose.

## 6. Relationship to Karry Kraze

Karry Kraze is an online e-commerce business.

The purpose of Karry Kraze is to generate business revenue through e-commerce operations. In the future, some or all of Karry Kraze’s ownership, profits, distributions, or assets may be directed to or owned by the intended future trust, subject to proper legal, tax, business, and estate planning guidance.

Karry Kraze should remain operationally separate from Justice McNeal LLC unless a formal structure is created that connects them. Any future transfer of ownership, profits, or assets from Karry Kraze to the intended future trust should be handled with professional review.

## 7. Property-Holding LLC Concept

The intended future trust may eventually own property-holding LLCs.

The concept is that each major property may be held in its own separate LLC. For example, a property located at “123 Tony Stark Road” could be owned by an LLC named “123 Tony Stark Road LLC.” That property LLC could then be owned by the intended future trust.

This structure may help separate liability, organize ownership, simplify recordkeeping, and protect the broader family legacy structure.

Each property-holding LLC should have its own records, accounts, insurance, tax documents, and operating agreement when appropriate.

The exact property ownership structure should be reviewed by an attorney, CPA, insurance professional, and real estate professional before being implemented.

## 10. Beneficiaries

The intended beneficiaries of the intended future trust may include descendants of the trustees, founders, or other family lines defined in the trust document.

Once the intended future trust is legally created, the trust agreement should clearly define who qualifies as a beneficiary, when they qualify, what rights they have, and what limitations apply.

The current concept is that beneficiaries may benefit from the intended future trust without automatically receiving direct ownership of trust assets.

For example, a property owned by a property-holding LLC may remain owned by that LLC, and the LLC may remain owned by the intended future trust. The property itself would not automatically be transferred directly to a beneficiary unless the trust document specifically allows it.

The goal is to preserve control, protection, and continuity of legacy assets, rather than automatically dividing and distributing major assets outright.

The intended future trust may also address how certain assets, such as life insurance proceeds, may be divided. For example, a life insurance policy could be structured so that a percentage goes to the intended future trust and a percentage goes to the insured person’s descendants, if legally and financially appropriate.

These beneficiary rules should be carefully drafted by an attorney.

## 12. Contribution Handling After Trust Creation

After the intended future trust is legally created, contribution rules may need to be updated.

The intended future trust may decide whether contributions continue to be collected through Justice McNeal LLC, directly by the trust, or through another approved structure.

Once created, contribution rules should define:

* Who may contribute
* How contributions are made
* Whether contributions are gifts
* Whether contributions are refundable
* Whether contributions create any access or participation status
* Whether contributions create any ownership or beneficiary rights
* How contributions are tracked
* How contributions are reported
* How contributions may be used
* Whether contribution amounts affect any rights or privileges

The current intention is that contributions are voluntary funds contributed through Justice McNeal LLC in support of the Justice McNeal Legacy Foundation mission but do not create ownership, equity, LLC membership rights, voting rights, trustee rights, beneficiary rights, repayment rights, withdrawal rights, guaranteed returns, or direct claims to trust assets unless legally documented otherwise.

## 14. Restricted Uses of Trust Assets

Once the intended future trust is legally created, trust assets should not be used in ways that conflict with the mission or weaken the long-term legacy structure.

Restricted uses may include:

* Personal spending unrelated to the mission
* Undocumented withdrawals
* Unapproved loans to family members
* Purchases that unfairly benefit one person over the broader trust mission
* High-risk speculation
* Gambling or betting
* Illegal, unethical, or misleading activity
* Commingling trust assets with unrelated personal assets
* Using trust property without approval
* Selling or transferring major assets without following the required decision-making process
* Any use that creates unnecessary risk, conflict, or harm to the trust structure

Specific restrictions should be finalized in the legal trust document.

## 15. Investment and Asset Philosophy

Once the intended future trust is legally created, it should be guided by long-term thinking, discipline, diversification, asset protection, and responsible stewardship.

The investment philosophy should prioritize long-term growth and preservation over short-term profit or high-risk speculation.

The intended future trust may invest in broad-market ETFs, long-term investment accounts, real estate, businesses, land, and other assets that align with the mission.

All investments carry risk, including the possible loss of value. The intended future trust should maintain clear records, seek professional guidance when appropriate, and avoid decisions that create unnecessary risk to the family legacy structure.

## 16. Decision-Making and Voting Concepts

After the intended future trust is legally created, future trust decisions should be made according to the trust document, LLC operating agreements, and any formal family governance rules adopted at that time.

The current concept is that major decisions may be made through a voting process among trustees or other authorized decision-makers.

When possible, major decisions may be discussed in person or through a documented meeting process.

Major decisions that may require voting or approval could include:

* Buying land
* Selling land
* Buying or selling property
* Creating or dissolving LLCs
* Appointing or removing LLC managers
* Changing investment strategy
* Making large withdrawals
* Approving large expenses
* Approving family use of trust assets
* Funding major projects
* Transferring business ownership
* Updating contribution policies
* Amending governance documents when allowed

As the intended future trust and related LLCs grow, the structure may include entity representatives or managers from different LLCs. These representatives may provide reports, recommendations, or votes if allowed by the trust document or governance rules.

Once created, the voting structure should define:

* Who gets to vote
* What decisions require a vote
* What percentage is needed for approval
* Whether some decisions require unanimous approval
* Whether some decisions can be made by a managing trustee
* How meetings are documented
* How conflicts of interest are handled
* What happens if there is a tie
* What happens if a trustee or decision-maker is unavailable

## 17. Amendment or Change Concepts

Once the intended future trust is legally created, the trust agreement should define whether and how it can be changed in the future.

The current concept is that changes should follow a formal process and may require trustee approval, voting, professional review, or other requirements stated in the trust document.

Possible changes may include:

* Updating trustee rules
* Updating beneficiary rules
* Updating contribution rules
* Updating investment rules
* Adding or removing entities
* Adding or removing trust powers
* Changing decision-making procedures
* Updating asset-use rules
* Updating distribution rules

The ability to amend or change the trust depends heavily on whether the trust is revocable, irrevocable, or structured in another way.

This section should be reviewed carefully by an attorney before the trust is created.
