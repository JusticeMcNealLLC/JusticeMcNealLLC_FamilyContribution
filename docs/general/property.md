## Why Container Home

We chose container over traditional for specific reasons that match our situation:

| Reason | Why It Fits Us |
|---|---|
| **Modular expansion** | Add containers as the family grows and funds allow |
| **Phased build** | Don't need to do it all at once |
| **Resale irrelevant** | Property stays in the family — we don't care about market value |
| **Cost potential** | Can be cheaper upfront depending on build approach |
| **Long-term vision** | We can keep adding — second unit, guest container, workshop, etc. |

### Container Home Expansion Model
```
Phase 1: 2 containers (parents' primary living space)
Phase 2: Add 1–2 more containers (extra rooms / family visiting space)
Phase 3: Add separate guest unit / additional structure
Phase 4: Full family compound with multiple structures on same land
```

### Container Home Challenges to Plan Around

These are real — not deal-breakers, but need to be researched for any specific county:

- [ ] **Zoning** — not every county allows container structures; confirm before buying land
- [ ] **Permits** — building permits required; container homes may have special requirements
- [ ] **Insulation** — metal conducts heat/cold; spray foam or interior insulation is usually required in Georgia climate
- [ ] **Utilities** — septic, well, and electric hookup costs are separate and can be significant
- [ ] **Financing** — lenders are less comfortable with container homes; may need construction loan or personal loan approach

## Family Skills as a Cost Advantage

| Skill | Who | Where It Helps |
|---|---|---|
| Tile work | Dad | Flooring, bathroom, kitchen finishing |
| Electrical | Brother-in-law | Wiring, fixtures, panel help |
| Mechanical / general repair | Brother-in-law | Equipment, structural help, maintenance |

### Why This Matters
Labor is often 30–50% of construction cost. Having family with real skills could dramatically reduce what we pay contractors — especially for finishing work that happens after the main structure is up.

### Important Rule
Any work that requires a license (especially electrical) should be handled by the licensed person or properly permitted. The goal is to save money legally, not create issues with inspections or future insurance.

## Loan Strategy

### The Reality of Lending to a New LLC

Banks and lenders are cautious with new LLCs because:
- No business income history
- No LLC credit history
- No existing assets in the LLC's name
- Construction loans are already higher risk

This means: **personal guarantees will almost always be required**, even if the LLC is the official borrower.

### Our Likely Loan Structure

```
Borrower:  Justice McNeal LLC
Guarantors: Justin + siblings (or willing family members)
```

This means the bank looks at:
- LLC (legal borrower)
- All guarantors' personal credit scores
- All guarantors' income and debt-to-income ratios
- Down payment and collateral

**Siblings do NOT need to be LLC members to be guarantors.** Being a guarantor is a separate legal commitment — it means "if the LLC can't pay, I will." The siblings can support the loan without owning the LLC.

### Using All 4 Credits

This is actually smart. If all 4 guarantee the loan:

- Bank has more confidence (4 income sources backing it)
- Weaker individual scores can be offset by stronger ones
- Combined income shows debt coverage ability

Working at Delta Community Credit Union is an additional advantage — strong member relationships, inside knowledge of how underwriting works, and potential comfort with a known borrower.

## Land: Cash vs Loan

### Why Buying Cash Was Suggested
- No lender restrictions on what you build
- No monthly debt payment
- Land loans can be hard to get for vacant land
- Simpler — you own it outright

### Why Paying Cash Is Complicated for Us
- Takes 5+ years to save $40k–$70k at current pace
- Depletes the portfolio significantly
- Leaves little money to actually build
- Kills momentum

### Our Position
We **do not want to drain the portfolio** to buy land outright. The portfolio should keep compounding while we work on the property plan.

**Our borrowing principle:**
> Do not deploy more than 20–30% of total portfolio value for any single move. Keep the fund working.

### Better Land Scenarios

**Option A — Land Loan**
- Borrow specifically for land (separate from construction)
- Put 20% down (e.g., $12k–$14k on a $60k plot)
- Monthly land payment might be $300–$400
- Keep investing the rest
- Later, pursue separate construction financing

**Option B — Find Lower-Cost Land Early**
- Rural Georgia has land available for $15k–$30k in some areas
- Buy cheaper land early
- Hold it while continuing to invest
- Build later when the fund is much stronger

**Option C — Combination**
- Find affordable land ($20k–$35k range)
- Small loan or family cash purchase
- Land held in LLC
- Build over time in phases as fund grows

## HELOC — Our Position

A HELOC is a line of credit secured by home equity (borrowing against the value of a home you already own).

**Why we're cautious:**
- Puts an existing home at risk as collateral
- Creates more debt tied to someone's personal property
- Not aligned with conservative early-stage wealth building
- Only makes sense if there's a very specific repayment plan

**Our call:** HELOC is not a core strategy for us right now. Not ruling it out forever, but it's not the move at this stage.

---

## Portfolio-Backed Borrowing (Future Strategy)

This is the idea of borrowing against the investment portfolio rather than selling it — keeping investments growing while using portfolio value as collateral.

**Why it's attractive:**
- Portfolio keeps compounding
- Don't need to liquidate to access capital
- Lower interest rates than unsecured loans in some cases

**The reality:**
- Fidelity's securities-backed lending is generally aimed at portfolios around $500,000+ in taxable assets
- Margin/collateral risk: if portfolio value drops, lender can force sales or require more equity
- Fidelity explicitly warns pledged securities can be sold if collateral requirements aren't met

**Our call:** This is a **Phase 3–4 tool**, not something we use now. It becomes relevant once the fund is genuinely large, not at the $600–$50,000 stage.

**Our working principle:**
> Leverage is the seasoning, not the meal. Build the base first.

## Government Assistance — Important Reality Check

### What we initially hoped
If the LLC owns the home and charges parents rent, they might still qualify for housing assistance programs.

### The Reality
Government assistance programs (Section 8, HUD, SSI housing assistance, etc.) often look **beyond the legal entity name** to assess:

- Who controls the entity
- Whether the landlord relationship is "arm's length" (unrelated parties dealing fairly)
- Whether the housing arrangement is considered genuine market housing
- Whether family members are the beneficial owners of the entity

**Just because an LLC owns the home does not automatically make it unrelated-party housing.**

Programs can trace ownership and may still treat it as family-controlled housing.

**Does a trust fix this?** Probably not automatically. If the trust's beneficiaries are the parents' children, programs can still determine it's family-controlled.

### Our Rule
> Do not make any financial assumption that depends on government housing assistance continuing as-is until the **exact program rules for that specific program** have been verified.

This must be checked with a housing advocate, attorney, or directly with HUD/program administrator before we rely on it in the plan.

## Pre-Buy Checklist (Before Purchasing Any Land)

- [ ] Confirm county zoning allows container/modular structures
- [ ] Confirm county allows owner-assisted construction
- [ ] Check building permit requirements for container homes
- [ ] Verify septic feasibility on the specific parcel
- [ ] Confirm well or water access options
- [ ] Verify electric utility availability / hookup cost
- [ ] Check if land is in a flood zone (affects insurance + financing)
- [ ] Confirm road access (easement, public road, etc.)
- [ ] Review deed restrictions or HOA if any (rare for rural land)
- [ ] Confirm LLC can hold title in Georgia (yes, it can)
- [ ] Get a land survey if not already surveyed
